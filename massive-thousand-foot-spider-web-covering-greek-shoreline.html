Creepy pics reveal massive 1,000ft-long spider web covering Greek shoreline after arachnid populatio

Publish date: 2024-08-09

INVADING spiders have spun an enormous web in Greece after infesting a 1,000ft stretch of shoreline.

Hotter than usual weather in the fishing port of Aitoliko, has led to an explosion of Tetragnatha spiders following a boom in mosquitoes - the arachnid army’s favourite food.

The scene in the small western Greek town is an arachnophobe’s worst nightmare, with millions of the critters creeping around its nests.

The spiders, which have strange elongated bodies, are able to scuttle faster on water than on ground and so prefer their coastal nest.

Experts say the webs, which are spun at this time of year but are much larger than normal, are used for mating as well as catching flies.

Maria Chatzaki, professor of molecular biology and genetics at Democritus University of Thrace, Greece said heatwaves, sufficient humidity and food paved the way for the booming population.


What are Tetragnatha spiders?

The name Tetragnatha is derived from Greek, tetra — a numerical prefix referring to four and gnatha meaning "jaw".

They are found all over the world although most thrive in warmer climates in the tropics and subtropics.

Commony, they are called stretch spiders because of their eerier elongated body form.

When disturbed they will stretch their front legs forward — while they jut their other six legs backwards.

This allows them to hide on blades of grass or similar elongated pieces of foliage like twigs and leaves.

One of the biggest and most common species is T. extensa.

It can be found near lakes, river banks or swamps.

Large numbers of individuals can often be found in reeds, tall grass or minor trees and shrubs.

They are so light and nimble that they are able to run over water and chose the water's edge as their preferred habitat.
