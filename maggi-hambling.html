Maggi Hambling Age & Early Life

Maggi Hambling was born in Sudbury, Suffolk, on October 23, 1945. Hambling had two siblings: an 11-year-old sister named Ann and a nine-year-old brother named Roger. Her younger brother had wished for a younger sibling, but he ignored the fact that his new sibling was a girl and taught her woodworking and “how to wring a chicken’s neck.” Hambling had a close relationship with her mother, who taught ballroom dance and had Hambling as her partner. Her artistic abilities were inherited from her father. She didn’t have the same relationship with her father as she did with her mother, but when he retired, she gave him some oil paints and discovered he had a talent for painting.

At the Amberfield School in Nacton, Hambling first studied art with Yvonne Drewry. She went on to study at the East Anglian School of Painting and Drawing at Benton End from 1960 to 1969, where she studied under Cedric Morris and Lett Haines, then at Ipswich School of Art (1962–64), Camberwell (1964–67), and lastly the Slade School of Art at UCL, where she graduated in 1969.

Career

  • Hambling is noted for her detailed land and seascape paintings, which include a well-known series of North Sea paintings.
  • The National Portrait Gallery in London includes a number of her portraits.
  • Hambling became the National Gallery’s first artist-in-residence in 1980, following which she created a series of portraits of comedian Max Wall.
  • Her portrait series has a strong female presence.
  • Professor Dorothy Hodgkin was painted by Hambling in 1985 for the National Portrait Gallery.
  • Hambling was chosen in May 2018 to construct a statue honoring Mary Wollstonecraft, the “foremother of feminism.” Since 2011, the Mary on the Green movement has been striving to create a permanent tribute to the philosopher and author of A Vindication of Women’s Rights. It unanimously chose Hambling for the sculpture. A figure – characterized as an everywoman – emerges from organic stuff in Hambling’s design. Wollstonecraft’s claim to be “the first of a new genus” inspired it. On the plinth is Wollstonecraft’s famous quote, “I do not wish women to have power over men, but over themselves.”
